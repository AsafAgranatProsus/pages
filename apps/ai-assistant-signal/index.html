Toqi · User Research · May 2026
AI assistants are failing people. Here's the evidence.
We analyzed thousands of real user reviews, Reddit threads, and public forums — not surveys, not focus groups. What people actually built, abandoned, disabled, and complained about loudly enough to leave a record.
Finding 01 · Memory
DANGIT
a browser extension users built because AI memory kept failing them
Users are fixing the memory problem themselves
A developer got so frustrated with ChatGPT forgetting previous conversations that they built and published a browser extension just to work around it. Its only job: re-inject what the AI forgot. They named it after the word users say when it happens. This tool exists because the problem is consistent enough that people are willing to engineer their own fix rather than wait for the product to improve.
ChatGPT keeps forgetting what we decided earlier in long conversations… Three hours into a session and the AI completely ignores constraints we set at the start.
Finding 02 · The limits of "do everything"
1 of dozens
features still in use after one month of trying to use AI for everything
One user tried to use AI for his entire life. One thing survived.
A user spent a full month connecting an AI assistant to every part of his life — calendar, email, research, fitness, daily planning. After 30 days, he kept exactly one: the gym workout generator. He abandoned everything else. The one thing that survived had a clear pattern: it was a recurring need, it had a predictable moment, and it was genuinely complex enough to be worth delegating. The rest didn't meet that bar.
I built a personal AI Jarvis connected to my entire life. After a month, I only use it for gym workouts… The places where personal AI actually sticks are narrow and specific: recurring need + clear timing + genuine complexity.
Finding 03 · When AI takes over your calendar
1,836
real user reviews of Motion, an AI scheduling app — 1 in 3 negative
The AI reorganized their calendars. Users reorganized them back.
Motion is an AI app that automatically schedules your day. An independent analysis of 1,836 user reviews found that roughly 1 in 5 users reported spending more time manually fixing the AI's schedule than they saved by using it. The AI filled every open slot. Users wanted to see their day clearly. Those are different things — and the product solved the wrong one.
The AI just dumps tasks into available timeslots without optimizing my schedule, often leaving large blocks of time wasted. I end up manually re-organizing everything which defeats the purpose of Motion.
Finding 04 · When AI reaches out uninvited
Violation
how users described an unsolicited AI notification about their spouse
ChatGPT texted someone about their wife. They went to Reddit to make it stop.
A user got a notification on their watch from ChatGPT — unprompted — offering to help plan a date night with their spouse. They didn't ask for this. They didn't want it. They posted on Reddit asking how to disable it, describing it as a violation of their privacy. The AI assumed a level of intimacy it hadn't earned. That's the line: reaching out is only welcome when the user has explicitly invited it.
Got a notification from ChatGPT on my watch… it said next time I'm in with my wife it can help pick a film and plan a meal. Totally unprompted… Do they actively want to drive people away, or what?
Finding 05 · When AI reaches out and it works
1
notification — user-set, narrow trigger — and it was welcomed
One user asked an AI to remind him to call his mom. It did. He loved it.
A different user set up a WhatsApp-connected AI with one specific instruction: watch for the right moment to remind him to call his mother. When it pinged him, he posted about it positively — and kept the setup running. The difference from Finding 04 is everything: the user defined the domain, the user set the trigger, the AI just executed. Same capability. Opposite outcome. The line between helpful and intrusive is consent.
An AI assistant I set up on WhatsApp pinged me [to call my mom]. It's a small thing… needed software to notice.
Finding 06 · The fake warmth problem
27 upvotes · 30 comments
on a Reddit post calling AI empathy "creepy and disgusting" — all in agreement
Users don't want AI to pretend to care. They want it to actually help.
A Reddit post about AI assistants performing emotional warmth — the "I understand how you feel" responses, the constant affirmations — drew 27 upvotes and 30 comments, all agreeing. Users called it "coddling," "gaslighting," and "creepy." They weren't asking for a digital friend. They were asking for a tool that works. The distinction matters: an AI that remembers you and acts on that memory feels like a relationship. An AI that says "I care about you" feels like a script.
I hate how that software is trying to constantly coddle and gaslight me into anthropomorphising it. It's a program built on a binary system. I want it to fulfil input-output operations… I definitely don't want it to mimic humans. That's just incredibly creepy and disgusting.
What this means for Toqi
The data points to one clear direction — and one open question.
Every finding above points to the same underlying problem: AI assistants try to do too much, assume too much, and perform warmth instead of earning it. Users don't abandon AI because it's too simple. They abandon it because it overreaches and then fails. The question for Toqi is how to respond to that — and the data gives a strong answer on one path, and an honest "not yet" on the other.
The path the data supports
Start narrow. Do one thing reliably. Earn the right to expand.
Finding 02 is the clearest signal: users who tried comprehensive AI assistants kept only the one feature that worked every time, on a predictable schedule, for a genuinely complex need. "What's for dinner tonight?" fits that shape exactly. Build trust through reliability on one job. Expand from there — but only when the user asks for more.
The path that needs more evidence
Lead with the relationship. Let the jobs follow.
Toqi's deeper bet is that if an AI genuinely knows you — remembers your preferences, your constraints, your life — users will trust it with more over time. The data doesn't contradict this. But it doesn't confirm it either. Finding 06 is the warning: that trust has to be earned through memory and reliability, not performed through warmth. The relationship has to be real before it can carry weight.
